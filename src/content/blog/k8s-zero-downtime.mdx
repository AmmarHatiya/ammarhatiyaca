Deploying to Kubernetes without downtime sounds simple until you actually try it under production traffic. Here is everything I learned from running deployments across 50+ microservices.

## The Basics: Rolling Updates

Kubernetes rolling updates replace pods one at a time, but the default settings are often too aggressive. Here is what I use for production services:

```yaml
strategy:
  type: RollingUpdate
  rollingUpdate:
    maxSurge: 1
    maxUnavailable: 0
```

Setting `maxUnavailable: 0` ensures there is always at least the desired number of healthy pods running. The trade-off is slower deployments, but for user-facing services, reliability beats speed.

## Readiness Probes: The Unsung Hero

Your readiness probe is the gatekeeper between your pod and production traffic. A misconfigured probe is the number one cause of deployment-related errors I have seen.

Key principles:
- **Check actual functionality**, not just "is the process running." Hit an endpoint that queries the database, checks cache connectivity, and validates config
- **Set `initialDelaySeconds` generously.** If your app takes 30 seconds to warm up, set it to 35. Being conservative here prevents serving errors
- **Separate readiness from liveness.** Liveness probes restart pods; readiness probes remove them from the load balancer. Conflating them causes cascading restarts under load

## Pod Disruption Budgets

PDBs tell Kubernetes how many pods can be down simultaneously during voluntary disruptions (node drains, cluster upgrades). Without them, a cluster autoscaler can evict all your replicas at once.

```yaml
apiVersion: policy/v1
kind: PodDisruptionBudget
metadata:
  name: api-pdb
spec:
  minAvailable: 2
  selector:
    matchLabels:
      app: api
```

For any service running 3+ replicas, I set `minAvailable` to N-1. It is the simplest thing you can do to prevent accidental outages.
